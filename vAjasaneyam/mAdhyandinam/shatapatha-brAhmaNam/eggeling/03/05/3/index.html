01

  1. The sacrifice is a man; it is a man for the reason that a man spreads (prepares) it. In being spread it is made just as large 1 as a man: this is why the sacrifice is a man.

02

  1. The Soma-cart (shed) is no other than his head, and has Vish·πáu for its deity 2. And because Soma is therein–Soma being havis (material for offering) for the gods–therefore it is called HavirdhƒÅna (receptacle of havis).

03

  1. The ƒÄhavanƒ´ya is no other than his mouth: hence, when he offers on the ƒÄhavanƒ´ya, it is as if he poured (food) into the mouth.

04

  1. The sacrificial stake is no other than his crest-lock; and the ƒÄgnƒ´dhrƒ´ya and MƒÅrjƒÅlƒ´ya 3 are his arms.

05

  1. The Sadas 4 (tent for the priests) is no other than his belly: wherefore they feed in the Sadas, for whatever food is eaten here on earth all that settles down here in the belly. And because all the gods sat (sad) in it therefore it is called Sadas: and so do these Brahmans of every family now sit therein.

06

  1. And the two fires which are behind 5 it are his feet. In being spread it is made just as large as a man: this is why the sacrifice is a man.

07

  1. The cart-shed has doors on both sides; and so has the Sadas doors on both sides: hence this man is perforated from one end to the other. He steps to the Soma-carts when they have been -washed down.

08

  1. They turn them round, the southern one on the south side, and the northern one on the north side 6. The larger of the two should be the southern (or right) one 7.

09

  1. Over them, having been turned round (and placed on the altar), they put a mat of reed-grass; or, if he cannot procure a reed-mat, a frame of split cane

made in like manner as a reed-mat. They fasten a front-band (to the posts of the front door 8). They enclose (the carts) within two upright hurdles; and lay a (second) reed-mat, or a frame of split cane made in like manner as a reed-mat, behind (the first mat 9).

10

  1. Now 10, having again entered (the hall), and

taken ghee in four ladlings, he makes offering to SavitrÃ• for his impulsion, for SavitrÃ• is the impeller (prasavitrÃ•) of the gods: ‘We will perform the sacrifice, for one impelled by SavitrÃ•,’ thus (the priest thinks and) therefore he makes offering to SavitrÃ•.

11

  1. He offers with the text (VƒÅj. S. V, 14; Rig-veda V, 81, 1), ‘They harness the mind and they harness the thoughts’–with the mind and with speech they truly perform the sacrifice. When he says, ‘They harness the mind,’ he harnesses the mind; and when he says, ‘and they harness the thoughts (dhƒ´),’ he harnesses speech; for it is thereby 11 that people seek to make their living in accordance with their respective intelligence (dhƒ´), either by reciting (the Veda), or by readiness of speech, or by songs,–with these two thus harnessed they perform the sacrifice.

12

  1. ‘The priests of the priest, of the great inspirer of devotion,’–the learned BrƒÅhmans versed in sacred writ, truly, are the priests: it is regarding them that he says this. And ‘of the great inspirer of devotion 12,’–the great inspirer of devotion, truly, is the sacrifice: it is regarding the sacrifice that he says this. ‘The knower of rites alone hath assigned the priestly offices,’ for, in performing

the sacrifice, they indeed assign the priestly offices. ‘Great is the praise of the divine SavitrÃ•; Hail!’ Thus he offers to SavitrÃ• for his impulsion.

13

  1. Having then taken ghee a second time in four ladlings, he walks out (of the hall by the front door). The (sacrificer’s) wife is led out by the south door. He then lays down a piece of gold in the right wheel-track of the southern Sonia-cart, and offers thereon, with (VƒÅj. S. V, 15; Rig-veda I, 22, 17), ‘Vish·πáu strode through this(universe),thrice he put down his foot: it is enveloped in his dust; Hail!’ The residue (of ghee) he pours into the wife’s hand. She anoints the burning (part) of the axle 13 with (VƒÅj. S. V, 17), ‘Audible to the gods, announce ye unto the gods!’ He hands to his assistant both the offering-spoon and the melting-pot. They lead the wife round by the back of the two fires 14.

14

  1. Having taken ghee in four ladlings, the assistant lays down a piece of gold in the right wheel-track of the northern Soma-cart, and offers thereon, with (VƒÅj. S. V, 16; Rig-veda VII, 99, 3), ‘Be ye too abundant in food and milch kine and pastures, through benevolence to man! Thou proppedst asunder these two worlds, O Vish·πáu; with beams of light didst thou hold fast 15 the earth on all sides; Hail!’ The residue (of ghee) he pours into the wife’s hand. She anoints the burning (part) of the axle with,

‘Audible to the gods, announce ye unto the gods!’ Then as to why he thus offers.

15

  1. Now, once on a time, the gods, while performing sacrifice, were afraid of an attack on the part of the Asura-Rakshas; and, the ghee being a thunderbolt, they kept off the evil spirits from the south by that thunderbolt, the ghee; and thus they came not after them on their way. And in like manner does he now keep off the evil spirits from the south by that thunderbolt, the ghee; and thus they do not come after him on his way. And the reason why he offers with two verses relating to Vish·πáu, is that the Soma-cart belongs to Vish·πáu.

16

  1. And in that the wife anoints the burning (part) of the axle, thereby a productive union is effected; for when woman and man become heated, the seed flows, and thereupon birth takes place. She anoints in a direction away (from the cart), for away the seed is cast. He then says (to the HotrÃ•), ‘Recite to the Soma-carts as they are wheeled forward 16!’

17

  1. He makes (the sacrificer) say, ‘Go ye both forward, furthering the cult!’ The cult, namely, is the sacrifice: ‘go ye both forward, furthering the sacrifice’ he thereby means to say. ‘Convey ye the sacrifice upward; lead it not astray 17!’ whereby he means to say, ‘convey this sacrifice upward to the world of the gods;’ and by saying ’lead it not astray,’ he prays for this (sacrificer) that he may not

stumble. Let them wheel (the carts) forward, as it were lifting them, so that they may not creak; for of the Asuras is that voice which is in the axle 18: ‘Lest the Asuras‚Äô voice should speak here!’ so he thinks. But if they should creak,–

18

  1. Let him make (the sacrificer) say this, ‘Speak ye unto your own cow-pen, ye divine resorts; speak not my life away, speak not my offspring away!’ This, then, is the expiation thereof.

19

  1. As to this they say, ‘Let him stride three steps from the high altar westward and make the Soma-carts stop there: this is the measure for the Soma-carts.’ But there is no (fixed) measure in this; wherever he himself may think fit in his mind, only not too near 19, nor too far (from the high altar), there let him stop them.

20

  1. He salutes them with, ‘May ye rejoice here on the height of the earth!’ for this (altar) is verily the height 20 (top) of the earth, since his offering-fire is in the heaven. He makes them rest on their naves 21 for that is the appearance of repose.

21

  1. The Adhvaryu, having gone round along the north side (of the carts), props the southern cart, with (VƒÅj. S. V, 18; Rig-veda I, 154, 1), ‘Now will I declare the heroic deeds of Vish·πáu, who measured out the earthly regions; who propped the upper seat, striding thrice, the wide-stepping! For Vish·πáu (I prop) thee!’ He fixes the prop in a different place from where (it is fixed) in ordinary practice 22.

22

  1. The assistant then props the northern cart, with (VƒÅj. S. V, 19), ‘Either from the heaven, O Vish·πáu, or from the earth, or from the great, wide airy region, O Vish·πáu, fill both thine hands with wealth and bestow on us from the right and the left! For Vish·πáu thee!’ He fixes the prop in a different place from where (it is fixed) in ordinary practice. The reason why he performs with prayers to Vish·πáu is that the Soma-cart belongs to Vish·πáu.

23

  1. He then makes (the sacrificer) say, after touching the middle reed-mat 23 (VƒÅj. S. V, 20; Rig-veda I, 154, 2), ‘Let Vish·πáu then be praised for his power, terrible like a wild beast prowling about the mountains, on whose three wide strides all beings abide!’ Now that (mat-covering) indeed is his (Vish·πáu, the shed’s) upper skull-bone 24

for thereon, as it were, the other skull-bones rest: this is why he says ’they abide on.’

24

  1. Thereupon he makes him say, after touching the front-band (VƒÅj. S. V, 2), ‘Thou art Vish·πáu’s fillet;’ for it indeed is his fillet. He then makes him say, after touching the two upright hurdles, ‘Ye are the corners of Vish·πáu’s mouth;’ for they indeed are the corners of his mouth. Then that mat which is behind there, that indeed is that skull-bone of his here behind (viz. the occiput).

25

  1. With ‘Thou art Vish·πáu’s sewer 25,’ he sews (the hurdles to the four door posts) with cord by means of a wooden pin. With, ‘Thou art Vish·πáu’s fixed (point) 26,’ he then makes a knot, ’lest it should fall asunder.’ That same (knot) he undoes when the work is completed; and thus disease 27 befalls not either the Adhvaryu or the Sacrificer. The completed (cart-shed) he touches with, ‘Thou art Vish·πáu’s own,’ for the Soma-cart (and shed) belongs to Vish·πáu.
